# Scholr User Validation Plan

## Goal

Run a focused 7-day validation sprint with 10 BTech students to learn whether Scholr saves time on real research, notes, and doubt-solving workflows.

## Success Metrics

- 10 students test Scholr
- 5 students return at least twice
- 3 students use more than one module
- 2 students explicitly say Scholr saves them time
- 1 student says they would pay for a stronger version

## 7-Day Plan

### Day 1
- Finalize the live product link and short intro message
- Create a simple tracker with student name, branch/year, modules used, and feedback summary

### Day 2
- Reach out to 10 target students across second, third, and final year
- Prioritize students actively preparing for exams, viva, mini-projects, or final-year topic selection

### Day 3
- Ask the first 3 to 4 students to use one real prompt in Research, one in Notes, or one in Doubt
- Watch for confusion around prompt quality, trust, and output readability

### Day 4
- Follow up with the remaining students
- Note repeat-use behavior and whether they naturally try a second module

### Day 5
- Group feedback into categories:
  - useful output
  - confusing UI
  - weak prompt/result fit
  - trust and verification concerns
  - missing workflow expectations

### Day 6
- Summarize patterns and identify the top three product improvements
- Decide whether the next milestone is prompt refinement, stronger persistence, or auth

### Day 7
- Write a short validation memo
- Update README, pitch notes, and resume bullet with actual usage signal if the feedback is positive

## Feedback Questions

Ask each student:

1. What topic did you try and which module did you use?
2. Did the result save you time compared with your normal workflow?
3. Was the structure clear enough for notes, viva prep, or research starting points?
4. What felt confusing, missing, or unreliable?
5. Would you use this again this week? Why or why not?
6. Which second module would you try next, if any?
7. If Scholr became more polished and reliable, would you consider paying for it?

## What To Look For

- Do students understand what each module is for without explanation?
- Do they trust the output enough to reuse it?
- Do they naturally try multiple modules?
- Do they mention speed, clarity, or reduced tab-switching as a benefit?
- Do they ask for login/history persistence, exports, or subject-specific tuning?
